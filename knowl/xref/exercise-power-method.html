Exercise 5.

We have seen that the matrix \(A = \left[\begin{array}{rr} 1 \amp 2 \\ 2 \amp 1 \\ \end{array}\right]\) has eigenvalues \(\lambda_1 = 3\) and \(\lambda_2=-1\) and associated eigenvectors \(\vvec_1 = \twovec{1}{1}\) and \(\vvec_2=\twovec{-1}{1}\text{.}\)
  1. Describe what happens when we apply the inverse power method using the initial vector \(\xvec_0 = \twovec{1}{0}\text{.}\)
  2. Explain why this is happening and provide a contrast with how the power method usually works.
  3. How can we modify the power method to give the dominant eigenvalue in this case?
Answer.
  1. We see that the vectors \(\overline{\xvec}_k\) do not converge and the multipliers \(m_k\) converge to the wrong value.
  2. The multipliers are obtained from the first component, then the second component, then the first, and so on.
  3. Choose one of the components, say, the first one and consider the ratio between that component of \(A\overline{\xvec}_k\) and \(\overline{\xvec}_k\) in place of the multiplier \(m_k\text{.}\)
Solution.
  1. We see that the vectors \(\overline{\xvec}_k\) do not converge but instead flip between approximations to \(\vvec_2\) and \(-\vvec_2\text{.}\) Also, the multipliers \(m_k\) are converging to \(1\) rather than \(-1\text{.}\)
  2. When applying the power method, the multipliers \(m_k\) are usually formed from the same component of the vectors \(\overline{\xvec}_k\) in every iteration. Here, we see that the multipliers are obtained from the first component, then the second component, then the first, and so on.
  3. Choose one of the components, say, the first one and consider the ratio between that component of \(A\overline{\xvec}_k\) and \(\overline{\xvec}_k\) in place of the multiplier \(m_k\text{.}\) The problem is that you must make sure that this component is not approaching zero.
