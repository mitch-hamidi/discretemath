Exercise 6.

Suppose that \(A\) is a stochastic matrix and that \(\xvec\) is a probability vector. We would like to explain why the product \(A\xvec\) is a probability vector.
  1. Explain why \(\xvec=\threevec{0.4}{0.5}{0.1}\) is a probability vector and then find the product \(S\xvec\text{.}\)
  2. More generally, if \(\xvec\) is any probability vector, what is the product \(S\xvec\text{?}\)
  3. If \(A\) is a stochastic matrix, explain why \(SA=S\text{.}\)
  4. Explain why \(A\xvec\) is a probability vector by considering the product \(SA\xvec\text{.}\)
Answer.
  1. \(S\xvec=[1]\text{.}\)
  2. If \(\xvec\) is a probability vector, \(S\xvec=[1]\text{.}\)
  3. The entries in \(SA\) are the sums of the columns of \(A\text{,}\) which are all \(1\text{.}\)
  4. \(\displaystyle S(A\xvec)=(SA)\xvec = S\xvec = [1]\)
Solution.
  1. Since the components of \(\xvec\) are nonnegative and add to \(1\text{,}\) \(\xvec\) is a probability vector. We see that \(S\xvec=[1]\text{.}\)
  2. Multiplying any vector \(\xvec\) by \(S\) just adds the components of \(\xvec\text{.}\) Therefore, if \(\xvec\) is a probability vector, \(S\xvec=[1]\text{.}\)
  3. Because the columns of \(A\) add to \(1\text{,}\) we see that \(S\) multiplying any column of \(A\) gives \(1\text{.}\) Consequently, the entries in \(SA\) are the sums of the columns of \(A\text{,}\) which are all \(1\text{.}\) This gives \(SA=S\text{.}\)
  4. Because all the entries in both \(A\) and \(\xvec\) are nonnegative, it follows that the components of \(A\xvec\) are nonnegative. Then we have \(S(A\xvec)=(SA)\xvec = S\xvec = [1]\text{,}\) which shows that the components of \(\xvec\) add to \(1\text{.}\) Therefore, \(\xvec\) is a probability vector.
